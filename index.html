How We Got the Bible

The Bible you hold in your hands today did not fall from the sky as a finished book. It was written over approximately 1,500 years by about 40 different authors ‚Äî kings, shepherds, fishermen, physicians, prophets, and scholars ‚Äî on three continents, in three languages, yet with one unified message. Understanding how these ancient writings traveled from their original manuscripts to your bedside table deepens our appreciation for the remarkable providence behind every page.

~250 B.C. ‚Äî The Septuagint (LXX)

Jewish scholars in Alexandria, Egypt translated the Hebrew Old Testament into Greek, creating the Septuagint. This became the Bible of Greek-speaking Jews and was the version most often quoted by the New Testament writers. It was a critical bridge between the Hebrew world and the Greek-speaking world into which Jesus was born.

~A.D. 45‚Äì95 ‚Äî The New Testament Written

The apostles and their close companions wrote the 27 books of the New Testament in Koine Greek. Paul's letters came first (starting ~A.D. 49), followed by the Gospels, Acts, the remaining epistles, and finally Revelation (~A.D. 95). The criteria included apostolic authorship (or connection), consistency with accepted doctrine, and widespread use in the churches.

A.D. 382‚Äì405 ‚Äî The Latin Vulgate

Jerome, one of the greatest scholars of the early church, translated the Bible from Hebrew and Greek into Latin ‚Äî the common language of the Western Roman Empire. His translation, the Vulgate, became the standard Bible of Western Christianity for over a thousand years. For centuries, this was the only Bible most Europeans ever heard.

A.D. 500‚Äì1500 ‚Äî The Bible Locked in Latin

For roughly a thousand years, the Bible was primarily available only in Latin. Ordinary people could not read it for themselves. The institutional church controlled interpretation. Scattered attempts were made at translation (e.g., portions in Old English by the Venerable Bede, ~A.D. 735), but it was dangerous ‚Äî even deadly ‚Äî to translate Scripture into the common tongue.

Brave men like Wycliffe, Tyndale, Coverdale, and the King James translators risked ‚Äî and sometimes gave ‚Äî their lives to put the Bible into the language of the people. Tyndale was strangled and burned at the stake in 1536 for his work. His dying prayer: "Lord, open the King of England's eyes." As of 2025, portions of the Bible have been translated into over 4,000 languages worldwide.

How We Got the Bible in English

From Hand-Copied Latin to Your Phone

~A.D. 735

Venerable Bede

Translated the Gospel of John into Old English on his deathbed. Among the earliest known translations of any Scripture into an English dialect.

üìö Bede
1382

Wycliffe's Bible

The first complete Bible in English, translated from the Latin Vulgate by John Wycliffe and his associates. Hand-copied by scribes. The Catholic Church condemned it and later exhumed and burned Wycliffe's bones.

üìö Wycliffe
1455

Gutenberg's Printing Press

Johannes Gutenberg printed the Latin Vulgate Bible ‚Äî the first major book printed with movable type. The press made mass production of Bibles possible for the first time in history, setting the stage for the Reformation.

üìö Gutenberg
1526

Tyndale's New Testament

William Tyndale produced the first printed English New Testament, translated directly from the Greek (not Latin). Smuggled into England in bales of cloth. Tyndale was betrayed, arrested, and executed in 1536. An estimated 80% of the KJV New Testament derives from Tyndale's work.

üìö Tyndale
1535

Coverdale Bible

Miles Coverdale produced the first complete printed English Bible. He drew on Tyndale's work plus German and Latin sources. The first English Bible to separate the Apocrypha into its own section.

üìö Coverdale
1537

Matthew's Bible

Published under the pseudonym "Thomas Matthew" (likely John Rogers). Combined Tyndale's OT and NT with Coverdale's work. The first English Bible to be officially licensed by the King.

1539

The Great Bible

Authorized by Henry VIII, this was the first Bible officially placed in every church in England. Revised by Coverdale. Its large size (over 16 inches tall) gave it the name "Great Bible."

1560

Geneva Bible

Produced by English exiles in Geneva, Switzerland. The first English Bible with numbered verses. Included extensive study notes with Calvinist theology. The Bible of Shakespeare, John Bunyan, and the Pilgrims who sailed on the Mayflower. Outsold the KJV for decades after its release.

üìö Geneva Bible
1568

Bishops' Bible

Produced by Church of England bishops to counter the Geneva Bible's Calvinist notes. Became the official pulpit Bible of the English church and served as the primary starting text for the KJV translators.

1582/1610

Douay-Rheims Bible

The first Catholic English translation, produced by English Catholic exiles in France. NT published in Rheims (1582), OT in Douay (1610). Translated from the Latin Vulgate. Revised by Bishop Challoner (1749‚Äì1752), remaining the standard Catholic English Bible for over 200 years.

üìö Douay-Rheims
1611

King James Version (KJV)

Commissioned by King James I and produced by 47 scholars organized into six panels. Drew on Tyndale, the Bishops' Bible, and the best Greek and Hebrew texts available. Its majestic language shaped the English tongue for over 400 years. The most printed book in history. Updated to modern spelling in 1769.

üìö KJV üìñ 1611 Original
1885

English Revised Version (ERV)

The first major revision of the KJV, produced by 50+ British and American scholars. Incorporated older and better Greek manuscripts discovered since 1611 (notably Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus). The American edition became the ASV (1901).

1952‚Äìnow

The Modern Translation Era

The RSV (1952) launched an explosion of modern translations. Hundreds now exist, each with different approaches, audiences, and emphases. Major milestones: NASB (1971), NIV (1978), NKJV (1982), NLT (1996), ESV (2001), CSB (2017), LSB (2021), and the ESV 2025 update.

A Timeline of Christian Denominations

From the First Church to Today

"There is one body, and one Spirit, even as ye are called in one hope of your calling; One Lord, one faith, one baptism, One God and Father of all, who is above all, and through all, and in you all."

‚Äî Ephesians 4:4‚Äì6

Christianity began with one church ‚Äî the community of believers who followed the risen Jesus Christ, empowered by the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2). Today, researchers estimate there are over 45,000 Christian denominations worldwide. How did we get from one church to thousands?

This timeline traces the major branches, splits, and new movements in Christian history. It identifies founders, dates, and the reasons each group emerged. Understanding this history helps us see that divisions often arose not because the Bible is unclear, but because human beings ‚Äî influenced by culture, politics, pride, and honest disagreement ‚Äî have interpreted the Bible differently across the centuries.

Why the Protestant Reformation Matters

For centuries, the Bible was available primarily in Latin, a language most ordinary people could not read. The Reformation, combined with the printing press, led to translations into German, English, French, and dozens of other languages. Martin Luther's German Bible (1534), William Tyndale's English New Testament (1526), and later the King James Version (1611) transformed the world.
The Reformers argued that no pope, council, or institution should have ultimate authority over a Christian's conscience ‚Äî only Scripture, illuminated by the Holy Spirit, holds that place. This is the principle of "Sola Scriptura" (Scripture alone). It did not mean each person invents their own truth; it meant the Bible itself is the standard by which all teaching must be tested ‚Äî exactly what the Bereans did in Acts 17:11.
The Reformers proclaimed that salvation is a free gift of God received through faith, not something earned by human works, payments, or rituals. "Sola Fide" (faith alone) and "Sola Gratia" (grace alone) became rallying cries rooted in Ephesians 2:8‚Äì9.
The Reformation's emphasis on individual Bible study, while freeing, also opened the door to countless competing interpretations. Without a single centralized authority, sincere believers reached different conclusions on baptism, the Lord's Supper, church governance, prophecy, and many other subjects. This is how one Reformation movement eventually became hundreds ‚Äî and then thousands ‚Äî of denominations. The Reformation was not a destination; it was a beginning.

Key Takeaways

1. Unity Was the Original Design

Jesus prayed "that they all may be one" (John 17:21). The first church was united. Divisions came later, through a mix of sincere disagreement, human sin, cultural differences, and political power. The desire for unity is still God's heart.

2. The Reformation Recovered Essential Truths

Its great gift was returning the Bible to the people and restoring the gospel of grace. Its unintended consequence was an explosion of competing interpretations. Both realities are part of the story.

3. No Human Institution Has All the Answers

Every denomination is made up of imperfect people with incomplete understanding. "For we know in part, and we prophesy in part" (1 Corinthians 13:9). Humility is the appropriate posture for every church and every Christian.

4. The Bible Should Make Us Better People

Jesus summarized the entire law in one sentence: "Therefore all things whatsoever ye would that men should do to you, do ye even so to them" (Matthew 7:12). If our study does not make us kinder and more loving, we have missed the point.

5. The Holy Spirit Is the Key

The same God who inspired the Scriptures must illuminate them. "When he, the Spirit of truth, is come, he will guide you into all truth" (John 16:13). The secret is not joining the right denomination ‚Äî it is opening your heart to God in prayer and being willing to follow wherever His truth leads.
